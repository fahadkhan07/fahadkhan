On June 17, 1971, President Richard Nixon stood before reporters and declared drug abuse "public enemy number one in the United States." That declaration, historians now widely agree, inaugurated not simply a policy initiative but a political realignment — one whose consequences we are still living with more than fifty years later.

The War on Drugs has been the most consequential domestic policy undertaking in modern American history. It has shaped the size and character of the prison system, determined the distribution of police power, structured the politics of crime and punishment, and — as John Ehrlichman's notorious 2016 confession confirmed — been designed, at least in part, to criminalize and disrupt political enemies.

Understanding why this policy has persisted despite overwhelming evidence of its failure requires grappling with something beyond rationality: the War on Drugs has never primarily been about drugs.

## Origins: Race, Politics, and the Construction of a Crisis

Ehrlichman, Nixon's domestic policy chief, put it plainly in an interview published in Harper's Magazine in 2016:

> "The Nixon campaign in 1968, and the Nixon White House after that, had two enemies: the antiwar left and Black people... We knew we couldn't make it illegal to be either against the war or Black, but by getting the public to associate the hippies with marijuana and Blacks with heroin, and then criminalizing both heavily, we could disrupt those communities."

Whatever one makes of this confession — and scholars debate its reliability — the historical record independently supports its core claim. Before the Controlled Substances Act of 1970, drug enforcement had long been entangled with racial politics. The Harrison Narcotics Act of 1914, the Marihuana Tax Act of 1937, and the Narcotic Control Act of 1956 were each accompanied by racialized moral panics that associated drug use with Black, Chinese, and Mexican populations respectively.

Nixon elevated and systematized this tradition, deploying drug enforcement as a mechanism of political and social control. The construction of a drug "crisis" served multiple purposes: it justified increased federal power, generated political support among white suburban voters, and provided a mechanism for disrupting left-wing organizing.

## Escalation: Reagan and the Architecture of Mass Incarceration

If Nixon built the War on Drugs, Reagan industrialized it. The 1980s saw drug enforcement expenditures multiply, mandatory minimum sentences codified into federal law, and the rhetoric of the "crack epidemic" mobilized to justify a spectacular expansion of incarceration.

The crack/powder cocaine sentencing disparity — in which possession of crack cocaine triggered sentences 100 times more severe than equivalent amounts of powder cocaine — became the defining legal expression of the racial politics of drug enforcement. Crack was associated with Black urban communities; powder cocaine with affluent white users. The disparity was not accidental.

By the end of the 1980s, the United States had the highest incarceration rate in the world. Drug offenses drove a disproportionate share of that growth. Between 1980 and 2010, the number of Americans imprisoned for drug offenses grew by more than 1,000 percent — from approximately 40,000 to over 500,000.

## What Has the War Accomplished?

By any serious policy metric, the answer is: very little, at great cost.

**Supply reduction** has been ineffective. Despite decades of interdiction efforts, drug prices have generally fallen while purity has increased. The heroin and fentanyl that now drive the opioid crisis are more available and more potent than at any point in the prohibitionist era.

**Demand reduction** through deterrence has proven largely illusory. Survey data consistently show that people who use drugs do so based primarily on social, psychological, and economic factors — not legal risk calculations. Criminalization does not significantly reduce drug use; it does reduce help-seeking behavior, drive drug use underground, and expose people who use drugs to greater harm.

**Public safety** has not been enhanced. The violence associated with drug markets — like the violence associated with alcohol Prohibition in the 1920s — is a product of the prohibition regime itself, not of drug use per se. Where drugs are legally regulated, the violence associated with illegal markets dissipates.

What the War on Drugs *has* accomplished is the systematic incarceration and civic exclusion of millions of Americans, disproportionately Black and Latino, often for conduct that poses little risk to public safety.

## Why Does It Persist?

If the policy has failed, why does it continue? Several explanations are worth considering.

**Institutional inertia**: The War on Drugs has created enormous institutional infrastructure — federal agencies, state programs, local police departments, private prison companies, prosecutors' offices — all with strong interests in perpetuating the status quo.

**Political incentives**: For most of the past half-century, being "tough on crime" has been politically profitable. Only recently has this calculus begun to shift, particularly on the left and, regarding marijuana, on the right.

**Racial politics**: Drug enforcement remains a mechanism — often implicit rather than explicit — of racial control. The communities most devastated by punitive drug policy have the least political power to resist it.

**Moral framing**: Drug prohibition is sustained, for many Americans, not by policy analysis but by moral conviction — the belief that drug use is sinful and should be punished. This moral framework is largely impervious to empirical evidence.

## Where Do We Go From Here?

The policy landscape has shifted meaningfully in recent years. Marijuana legalization has spread to more than half of U.S. states. The opioid crisis — which has disproportionately affected white communities — has produced, however fitfully, movement toward treatment-centered approaches. Sentencing reform, particularly regarding crack/powder disparity reduction, has garnered bipartisan support.

But these reforms have been partial, incremental, and unevenly distributed. They have not fundamentally challenged the punitive drug enforcement paradigm; they have modified it at the margins.

A genuine alternative requires rethinking the foundations: moving from punishment to public health, from criminalization to regulation, from mass incarceration to community investment. The evidence base for this alternative — from Portugal's decriminalization, to Swiss heroin prescription programs, to North American supervised consumption facilities — is substantial.

Whether the political will to pursue it can be assembled remains the central question of drug policy for the generation ahead.
